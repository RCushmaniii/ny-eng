## You Already Lead. The Room Just Doesn't Know It Yet.

You can run a meeting in Spanish with precision. You challenge ideas without apology. You give feedback that lands. You know how to hold a room.

Then the call switches to English — and something shifts.

You slow down. You hedge. You add "sorry" to sentences that don't need it. You ask if your question makes sense rather than just asking it. The authority you spent years building seems to dissolve the moment you change languages.

This is one of the most common and least talked-about challenges facing bilingual women executives in Mexico. And it has nothing to do with how well you speak English.

---

## Why the Language Switch Costs You Authority

The reason this happens is psychological, not linguistic.

You learned English in contexts designed to make you subordinate — classrooms, textbooks, grammar drills. Every time you were corrected, you learned that English was a place where you could be wrong. Where you needed permission. Where the native speaker was the authority and you were the student.

In Spanish, you built your voice over years of real professional experience. You learned to lead *in* the language while leading. The two developed together.

When you switch to English, your brain reaches for the language patterns it learned — and those patterns were formed in rooms where someone else held the power.

That's not a language problem. That's a conditioning problem.

---

## The Double Authority Challenge

Bilingual women executives in Mexico face something most coaching programs never address directly: you're not fighting one authority gap — you're fighting two simultaneously.

The first is the one every woman in leadership navigates: being taken seriously in rooms that weren't originally designed for you. The second is the language gap — the way English can strip away the tonal precision, the calibrated directness, and the earned confidence you've built in Spanish.

When these two challenges overlap, the effect compounds. A man with imperfect English still sounds authoritative if his message is clear and his posture is confident. A woman with imperfect English gets assessed differently — her credibility takes a harder hit, her hesitation is read differently, her softened language is categorized faster.

The stakes are not equal. Which means the solution can't be generic.

## The 4 Moments Where English Authority Breaks Down

If you've experienced any of the following, you're not alone:

**1. Opening a meeting**
In Spanish: "Empecemos — tengo tres puntos que necesito que resuelvan hoy."
In English: *"Um, so… should we maybe get started? I have a few things I wanted to go over if that's okay with everyone…"*

**2. Pushing back on an idea**
In Spanish: "Eso no va a funcionar. Aquí está la razón y aquí está lo que sí funciona."
In English: *"I mean, I'm not sure, it could work… I just wonder if maybe… I don't know, what does everyone else think?"*

**3. Delivering feedback**
In Spanish: "Este informe necesita otro nivel. Te voy a decir exactamente qué cambiar."
In English: *"So, this is really good, I just think maybe there are a couple of small things that could possibly be a little stronger…"*

**4. Saying no to a US stakeholder**
In Spanish: "Eso no está en el alcance. Si quieren eso, necesitamos renegociar el contrato."
In English: *"That's such a great idea, I just want to make sure I understand what you mean, because I think maybe we might need to look at the timeline a little bit more…"*

The ideas are the same. The authority isn't.

## What Executive English Actually Sounds Like

The difference between professional English and executive English isn't vocabulary. It's structure, economy, and posture.

| Situation | Weak (Student Voice) | Strong (Executive Voice) |
|-----------|---------------------|--------------------------|
| Opening a meeting | "Should we maybe get started?" | "Let's get started. Three things on the agenda today." |
| Disagreeing | "I'm not sure I agree, but maybe I'm missing something…" | "I see it differently. Here's why." |
| Setting a boundary | "I just want to make sure we're aligned on the timeline…" | "That's outside our current scope. If the scope changes, so does the timeline." |
| Giving feedback | "This is really good — I just wonder if maybe…" | "Strong start. Here's what needs to change before this is ready." |

Notice what's missing from the executive column: hedges, apologies, permission-seeking, and filler. What's present: directness, structure, and conviction.

These are learnable. More importantly, they're *already how you think* — in Spanish. The work is transferring the executive voice you've already built into your second language.
