There is a number that quietly determines who reaches the senior level, and most professionals have never heard it.

Sylvia Ann Hewlett's research at Coqual surveyed nearly 4,000 college-educated professionals, including 268 senior executives, and produced one of the most-cited findings in modern leadership research: **executive presence accounts for roughly 26% of what gets a leader promoted to the next level**. Not 26% of *one* factor. 26% of the entire promotion decision, full stop. (Hewlett, *Executive Presence*, HarperBusiness; updated in *Executive Presence 2.0*, 2023; *Harvard Business Review*, January 2024.)

That number is the entire reason this article exists.

If you are weighing whether to invest in elevating your executive English, the question you are really asking is: *does this move the needle on my career, or is it polish?* The honest answer, supported by decades of research, is that it moves the needle. Often more than the technical skill you have spent years developing.

This is not motivation. This is the data.

---

## What "executive presence" actually means

The phrase gets misused. Executive presence is not posture, suit choice, or a deeper voice. In Hewlett's framework — the most rigorous one available — it has three components: **gravitas (~67%)**, **communication (~28%)**, and **appearance (~5%)**. Communication is the second-largest component, and unlike gravitas, it is the most directly trainable. Within communication, "superior speaking skills" and "ability to command a room" lead the rankings.

Strip the corporate language away and what remains is concrete: executive presence is the linguistic signal you send under pressure. It is how you sound when stakes are high, time is short, and the people in the room are deciding whether to trust your judgment.

For a non-native English speaker operating in a global business, that signal travels through your second language. Which means your English is not a soft skill. It is the channel your authority moves through.

## The hidden tax of weak language

The cost of "weak" language at the senior level is not theoretical. It has been measured.

In 1980, linguists William O'Barr and Bowman Atkins published a study of courtroom witnesses showing that *low-status* speech — hedges, hesitation, intensifiers, tag questions — measurably reduced perceived authority and credibility, regardless of who was speaking. The original framing was about gender, but their data corrected it: the pattern was status-based, not gendered. High-status witnesses (physicians, professionals) used direct, structured speech. Low-status witnesses softened, qualified, and hedged. Subsequent experimental work through the 1990s and 2000s confirmed the effect: messages delivered in "powerless" style were rated as displaying "a reduced sense of control and authoritativeness," and in mock employment interviews, that style significantly reduced hiring recommendations.

The implication is uncomfortable. The instinct most professionals develop in their second language — softening, qualifying, adding "I think maybe," over-explaining — is the exact pattern listeners interpret as low-status. Your competence does not show through. Your hesitation does.

Filler words add their own measurable tax. A study analyzing sales calls found that success rates dropped sharply once filler words ("um," "uh," "like") exceeded **1.3% of total words spoken** (Rosenblum et al., *Advances in Physiology Education*, 2022). Earlier work out of Cal Poly's Communication Studies department (Gikas & Sutcliffe) found the same pattern across professional settings: filler density correlates inversely with perceived competence and credibility, regardless of who is speaking.

These are small linguistic habits. The career cost compounds for years.

## The four signals that quietly cost you the room

Senior leaders who command rooms are not the loudest, the most charismatic, or the most polished. They are the ones who have learned to suppress four specific signals that mark someone as not-quite-ready:

**Emotion.** Visible reactivity reads as loss of control. Peer-reviewed research links cognitive reappraisal — the ability to reframe rather than react — to higher leadership performance ratings from both supervisors and peers (Lopes et al., *Frontiers in Psychology*). The senior leader does not say "this is a disaster." They say "this is impacting performance and requires adjustment." Same reality. Different signal.

**Weakness.** "I'm struggling with this" is a true statement. It is also a signal that downgrades you in the listener's mental model. The executive equivalent — "this is currently a challenge that needs to be addressed" — communicates the same fact while preserving authority. The skill is not denial. It is *framing*.

**Unpreparedness.** "I'm not sure yet" sounds honest, but in a senior meeting it reads as unprepared. "The analysis is still being finalized" communicates the same uncertainty without the credibility cost. Executives are allowed to not know. They are not allowed to *sound* unprepared.

**Deference where authority is required.** "Can you take a look when you have time?" gives away control. "This requires your review" preserves it. The most consistent pattern across senior leaders is that they do not soften where the situation calls for direction. Softening is not politeness — it is a quiet transfer of authority the moment did not ask you to make.

These four shifts are not personality. They are habits — and habits are trainable.

## The non-native English tax is real. It is also solvable.

For non-native English speakers, there is an additional layer of evidence worth confronting directly.

A 2024 meta-analysis in *Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin* (Spence, Hornsey, Stephenson, & Imuta) synthesized accent-bias hiring research and found that standard-accented candidates were significantly more likely to be hired than non-standard-accented ones — with the effect "particularly pronounced for foreign accents." Earlier University of Chicago work (Lev-Ari & Keysar, 2010, *Journal of Experimental Social Psychology*) found listeners rated identical factual statements as *less truthful* when spoken with a foreign accent, attributed to "processing fluency" — the listener's brain works harder, and that effort gets misread as suspicion of the speaker.

Tsedal Neeley's five-year study at Harvard of Rakuten's English-mandate transition (650 interviews across eight countries, published as *The Language of Global Success*, Princeton University Press, 2017) documents the same dynamic at the organizational level. Even when global firms officially adopt English as their working language, non-native speakers experience systematic disadvantage in influence, perceived competence, and advancement *unless they actively close the language gap*. Native speakers gain unearned status. Non-natives must consciously cross the threshold to compete for senior roles.

This is not fair. It is, however, the data. And the same data shows the constraint is solvable: the disadvantage applies to *unaddressed* fluency gaps. Closing them — specifically at the executive register — restores the playing field.

## The myth that's holding you back

Before going further, one piece of folklore deserves a clean kill.

You have probably heard that "93% of communication is nonverbal" — the so-called "55/38/7 rule." It comes from Albert Mehrabian's 1967 studies. Mehrabian himself has spent decades trying to retract it: *"Unless a communicator is talking about their feelings or attitudes, these equations are not applicable."* His original experiments measured the resolution of *emotional incongruence* between word and tone on single words like "thanks." They were never designed to describe general communication.

If you have been told that words barely matter, the research disagrees. Words carry the signal. Tone and structure modulate it. The language you choose under pressure is not 7% of the message — it is the message.

## The second-order skill: adapting register without losing authority

The professionals who advance fastest add one more layer on top of executive style: they adjust register depending on the audience without diluting their authority.

Communication researchers Roderick Hart and Don Burks named this *rhetorical sensitivity* in 1972 (*Speech Monographs*) — the ability to "undergo the strain of adaptation" while preserving message integrity. Andrew Molinsky at Brandeis later quantified the cognitive cost of cross-cultural code-switching (*Academy of Management Review*, 2007), and Anicich and Hirsh (2017, *AMR*) documented how middle-power roles specifically require leaders to switch register vertically — deferential up, directive down, peer-level lateral — without falling into "behavioral inhibition."

In practice this means: how you brief the board is not how you talk to your engineering team, which is not how you negotiate with a vendor, which is not how you reassure a client whose project just slipped. The frame stays consistent — calm, structured, decision-oriented. The register flexes. Leaders who can do this fluently in English open doors that single-register communicators never see.

This is where most "advanced English" training stops short. Vocabulary and grammar do not teach you when to compress and when to expand, when to push and when to defer, when to be the room's structure and when to be its diplomat.

## What changes when you close the gap

The professionals who have done this work consistently report the same shift: they stop being talked over, the ideas they have always had start landing the first time they say them, and they get pulled into the conversations that *shape* decisions rather than the ones that execute them.

That is not a soft transformation. That is the 26% Hewlett's research identified — moving in your favor instead of against you.

If your English is the ceiling on what you have built, the data is unambiguous: it is worth raising.
