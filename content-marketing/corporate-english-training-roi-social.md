## LinkedIn Post

Your CFO doesn't care that your management team's CEFR scores improved.

When the budget meeting rolls around and someone in finance starts asking pointed questions about why the L&D line jumped, "improved fluency" doesn't land. What lands is money.

I just published the framework HR managers can use to defend corporate English training spend in financial terms — reframing it as operational infrastructure (CRM, ERP, security audits — non-discretionary investments evaluated against the cost of *not* having them), not employee development (discretionary, first to be cut).

The math, anchored to realistic Mexican-market numbers:

**Cost of weak English at the leadership level:**
→ 2-3 cross-border deals/year close more slowly or with worse margins → ~700K-2M MXN annually
→ Senior leaders spending 8-10 hours/week as de facto translators → 720K+ MXN of misallocated executive time
→ Rework from cross-border miscommunication → 400K-800K MXN
→ Hidden cost of senior leaders who can't be promoted to international roles → strategic compounding constraint

**Total typical annual cost: 2-3.5M MXN at the executive level.**

**Cost of a serious 12-week program for 4 senior leaders:** 80-150K MXN.

Even if the training captures only 10-20% of the cost gap, it pays for itself many times over in the first year — assuming the vendor can actually produce behavior change. That last part is where most companies lose, which is why the post links to a separate vendor-vetting framework.
