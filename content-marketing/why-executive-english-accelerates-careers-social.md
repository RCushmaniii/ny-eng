## LinkedIn Post

There's a number that quietly determines who reaches the senior level, and most professionals have never heard it.

**26%.**

That's the share of every promotion decision that hinges on what researchers call "executive presence" — based on Sylvia Ann Hewlett's Coqual study of nearly 4,000 professionals, including 268 senior executives. Not 26% of *one* factor. 26% of the entire decision (HBR, January 2024).

For non-native English speakers in global firms, that 26% travels through your second language. Which means your English isn't a soft skill. It's the channel your authority moves through.

I just published the research-backed case for why polished executive English measurably accelerates careers — and what to do about it.

The core findings, all from peer-reviewed work:

→ **Powerless speech** (hedges, hesitation, "I think maybe…") measurably reduces perceived authority and credibility, regardless of who's speaking. Your competence doesn't show through. Your hesitation does. (O'Barr & Atkins, 1980; replicated for 40+ years.)

→ **Filler words have a quantified threshold:** sales call success drops sharply once "um/uh/like" exceeds 1.3% of total words spoken (Rosenblum et al., 2022).

→ **Foreign-accented speakers are systematically rated as less credible and less truthful** — even when content is identical. Listeners' brains work harder, and that effort gets misread as suspicion. (Spence et al., 2024 meta-analysis; Lev-Ari & Keysar, 2010.)

→ **Tsedal Neeley's five-year Harvard study at Rakuten** (650 interviews, 8 countries): even when global firms officially adopt English, non-native speakers experience systematic disadvantage in influence and advancement *unless they actively close the language gap*.

The four signals that quietly cost senior professionals the room:
1. **Emotion** — visible reactivity reads as loss of control
2. **Weakness** — "I'm struggling with this" downgrades you in the listener's model
3. **Unpreparedness** — "I'm not sure yet" reads as unprepared in a senior meeting
4. **Deference where authority is required** — "Can you take a look?" gives away control

These aren't personality. They're habits. And habits are trainable.
