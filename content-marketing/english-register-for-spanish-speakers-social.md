## LinkedIn Post

You're on a board update with your US parent company. Your English is fine — better than fine; you've been working in it for fifteen years. But forty seconds in, you notice your sentences have shrunk. You're answering questions instead of guiding the conversation. The diplomacy and calibration you'd deploy effortlessly in Spanish have flattened into "Yes. We will address that."

**Your English didn't break. The controls did.**

In Spanish, register lives in your grammar — tú vs. usted, the subjunctive as a softening device (*quisiera, podría, hubiera*), the conditional cushion. You've been switching registers effortlessly for thirty years. The intuition is wired in.

In English, those controls don't exist. The same calibration work is done through completely different mechanics — and nobody ever taught them to you, because if you'd grown up in English you would have absorbed them by osmosis the same way you absorbed *tú* and *usted*.

I just published the missing manual. The four English mechanics that replace what Spanish does grammatically:

→ **Modal verbs** — "Send the report" / "Could you send the report?" / "Would it be possible to have the report?" Same content, three positions on the formality gradient. The grammar didn't change — only the modal stack did.

→ **Hedging density** — Senior English communicators rarely strip hedges entirely; they vary the *density* by context. A board update has lower hedging than a deferential request to a senior leader. Spanish-to-English one-to-one mapping is the most common mistake.

→ **Indirectness via question form** — English upper-register communication leans heavily on the interrogative-as-request. Senior leaders almost always frame the decision as a question that contains the answer.

→ **Lexical formality** — *Address* vs *fix*. *Consider* vs *think about*. *Implement* vs *put in place*. Latinate vocabulary reads as more formal; Anglo-Saxon reads as warmer. Paradoxically *easier* for Spanish speakers — the Latinate words are often near-cognates.

The two failure modes when these mechanics aren't trained:
- **Too stiff** (translating *usted* into a formality blanket — every email reads like a legal document)
- **Too casual** (defaulting to flat neutral register everywhere because the formality gradient feels invisible)

Both feel safe to the speaker. To the senior English-speaking listener, both signal disengagement.

## Spanish (LinkedIn / Facebook)

Estás en una junta de actualización con la matriz en Estados Unidos. Tu inglés está bien — más que bien; llevas quince años trabajando en él. Pero a los cuarenta segundos, notas que tus oraciones se han encogido. La diplomacia y la calibración que desplegarías sin esfuerzo en español se han aplanado en un "Yes. We will address that."

**Tu inglés no se rompió. Los controles sí.**

En español, el registro vive en tu gramática — tú vs. usted, el subjuntivo como dispositivo suavizador (*quisiera, podría, hubiera*), la almohadilla del condicional. Has estado cambiando de registro sin esfuerzo durante treinta años. La intuición está cableada.

En inglés, esos controles no existen. El mismo trabajo de calibración se hace a través de mecánicas completamente distintas — y nadie te las enseñó, porque si hubieras crecido en inglés las habrías absorbido por ósmosis igual que absorbiste *tú* y *usted*.

Acabo de publicar el manual que faltaba. Las cuatro mecánicas del inglés que reemplazan lo que el español hace gramaticalmente:

→ **Verbos modales** (apilado de could/would/might)
→ **Densidad de atenuantes** (varía por contexto)
→ **Indirección vía forma de pregunta**
→ **Formalidad léxica** (latino vs. anglosajón)

Los dos modos de falla cuando estas mecánicas no se entrenan: **demasiado rígido** (traducir *usted* en una manta de formalidad — cada correo se lee como documento legal) o **demasiado casual** (asentarse en un registro medio neutro para todo).
